Our Mission

GHO aims to contribute to the enabling environment for accelerating action against non communicable diseases and women’s health, especially cervical cancer.
One major focus includes innovative approaches, new tools and partnerships in this objective, and in other health related issues.

Major Areas of Interest

Our work is focused on three major areas of interest, with people at the center.

Women's Health

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) provide a basis to develop innovative approaches to improve women’s healthcare programs, particularly cervical cancer control. There are many new medications, vaccines, medical devices, diagnostic tests and new communication technologies now available to help these developments advance.

International Geneva

GHO creates synergies with some of the key actors of this unique concentration of international organizations including Permanent Missions, NGOs and Academia. Beyond this global platform, GHO will also develop relationships with Swiss research institutes, other NGOs and academia as well as other Swiss or European stakeholders involved in GHO’s health related topics to make the best use of the current trajectory on these issues.

Information and Communication Technologies (ICTs)

Especially mobile phone technology as a key strategy for advancing women’s health in both developed and developing countries, particularly in Africa where access and costs are major concerns. Those technologies can not only extend better health to women, but it can also bring it to the most affordable level , playing a key role in improving data collection, standards, monitoring and evaluation. Consequently, connected health should play an increasing role in improving cervical cancer control and the health care system as a whole.

Mainstreamed into existing healthcare structures

Governments have to create an enabling environment to scale up current pilot projects, such as m-Health, to extend community outreach and access to screening or to HPV vaccination uptake. This strategy must be mainstreamed into existing health care structures, agencies, and national health authorities’ policy and priorities frameworks. For the global community including donors and institutional players, it is essential that monitoring and evaluation is planned and budgeted for within programs to ensure sustainability beyond a successful pilot phase.

The best use of innovations to advance cervical cancer

Many pilot projects are considered as a laboratory for some of the most exciting innovations in cervical cancer control, incorporating new practices in mobile health, such as evidence based medicine, electronic medical records, financial and marketing innovations. -Health technology also revolutionizes the health care industry through innovative partnerships allowing a sustainable and scalable business model appropriate for large-scale implementations.

Using m-Health to extend access

m-Health has a tremendous impact on increasing local communities’ access to prevention and vaccination, screening and care and thus, reinforcing cervical cancer control implementation, through, among others, community mobilization, capacity building, collect of data and quality control. As this innovative technology brings in valuable strategic partnerships to support scale up, leveraging the network operator’s technical capacity and core competencies, it should greatly help accelerate action.

Identifying appropriate indicators

There is still a need for more methodologies for measuring impact of the new technologies on access, quality of care and health outcomes, such as ICts, Our reflection will include the search for appropriate indicators, bringing together all the stakeholders involved in cervical cancer field projects.

Mainstreamed into existing healthcare structures

In 2016, nearly 90% of the world's population subscribed to at least one cell phone. The mobile penetration in Africa is among the highest in the world, which shows that the continent is ready to take advantage of this digital revolution and with it, overcome its current health challenges. With basic training and mobile phones, local health community workers can be the health service delivery system. As such, our reflection will seek to better understand how we can capitalize on this growth to advance cervical cancer control in Africa. In addition, GHO will showcase m-Health pilot projects, their impact and the sustainable solutions for their scaling up beyond the pilot phase.
Worldwide, the increasing use of ICTs to improve the quality of life of the greatest number of people is very promising. We will place a particular emphasis on the elderly as those technologies provide them specific benefits, allowing them to better control their health, while staying at home, in connection with their medical services and relationships.
However, connected health is not easy sets of simple solutions to be implemented; there are many challenges and barriers for deploying those technologies in the field. Data and interoperability standards must also be put in place to ensure that health information being collected is fed back into the system and informs resource allocation decisions. Thus, new partnerships with all stakeholders, public and private, each of vital importance, are necessary to the enhancement and support of women’s health initiatives. The experiences from the field showcase how significant a role all of those collaborations are to scaling up current and future projects in e-Health.
